Money & Credit Cards
Use the right card and pay zero fees. Use the wrong one and lose 3% on everything.
The most important thing to know Standard US credit cards charge a 3% foreign transaction fee on every purchase. On a $5,000 trip, that's $150 in fees for nothing. Use any of the cards below and pay exactly $0 in foreign transaction fees.

The Chip + PIN issue (important for Europe) US cards use chip + signature. Most Paris restaurants and shops are fine with this. However, some self-service machines (Metro ticket kiosks, unattended parking, some museum ticket machines) require chip + PIN. Always carry some cash as backup for kiosk transactions.

ATM Strategy

  • Best debit card for ATMs: Charles Schwab Bank High Yield Investor Checking — reimburses ALL ATM fees worldwide, every month. If you don't have this account, get it before the trip.
  • Use bank ATMs only — not standalone ATMs in tourist areas (airports, attractions). Use BNP Paribas, Société Générale, or Crédit Agricole ATMs.
  • Never use your credit card at an ATM — cash advances carry 20%+ APR starting the moment of withdrawal.
  • Always choose to pay in euros (not dollars) when the ATM or terminal offers "Dynamic Currency Conversion." Choosing dollars lets the machine set the rate — always worse.
  • Cash to carry: €150–200 total for the week — markets, wine bars, tips, and any kiosk purchases. Everything else goes on card.

Never Do These

  • Airport currency exchange booths — typically 8–12% worse than mid-market rate. The Travelex at CDG is especially bad.
  • Hotel currency exchange — similar poor rates.
  • Traveler's checks — obsolete, ignored by most vendors.
  • Paying in USD when offered (Dynamic Currency Conversion) — always choose EUR.

American in Paris — Dos & Don'ts
What the guidebooks don't always say clearly.

✓ Do These

  • Say Bonjour the moment you walk into any shop, café, or museum — before anything else. It is expected and the tone of your entire interaction depends on it.
  • Say Merci on exit and Au revoir. These are not optional courtesies — they are basic social requirements.
  • Use S'il vous plaît with every request. "Excuse me" is not an opener in France; "Bonjour, s'il vous plaît…" is.
  • Make an attempt at French even if you only know five words. The attempt is more important than the result.
  • Sit down for coffee. Café au comptoir (standing at the bar) is cheaper and faster; café en salle (seated) is the experience. Know which you want and commit.
  • Tip modestly. Service is included (service compris) in the bill by law. Leaving €2–5 on a dinner table is appreciated but not expected. Tipping like you're in New York is unnecessary.
  • Keep your voice at the ambient level of the room. American volume in a French restaurant is noticed.
  • Dress well. Parisians dress with care even for casual occasions. You are not required to wear couture — just make an effort. A sundress is fine; athletic wear to dinner is not.
  • Carry your hotel card. The address in French saves you in any taxi or emergency.
  • Download offline Google Maps for Paris before you land. Saves data and works in the Metro.

✗ Don't Do These

  • Don't open with "Do you speak English?" — open with Bonjour, make an attempt, then ask. The difference in response is significant.
  • Don't ask for a doggie bag. Requesting takeout of a restaurant meal is culturally awkward in France. Either finish it or leave it.
  • Don't tip 20%. Service is already included. Tipping 20% at a Paris café reads as performative and slightly odd.
  • Don't use airport currency exchange booths. Ever. The rate is 8–12% worse than your credit card.
  • Don't hail unlicensed taxis, especially at CDG and tourist sites. Use G7 or Bolt app only.
  • Don't leave your phone on the restaurant table. It will be gone. Keep it in your bag.
  • Don't wander Gare du Nord or the surrounding streets at night without purpose. Be deliberate if passing through.
  • Don't engage with clipboard scams — people approaching with clipboards asking you to sign a petition or answer a survey. Walk away without stopping. It's a distraction for pickpockets.
  • Don't pay in dollars when the card machine asks — always choose EUR (see Dynamic Currency Conversion).
  • Don't book anything through third-party resellers for museums — only official museum websites. Fake tickets are sold in July.

Scams to Know & Ignore

  • The clipboard petition: Person approaches with a clipboard, asks you to sign for a cause. While you're distracted, accomplice picks your bag. Walk past without engaging.
  • The gold ring: Someone "finds" a gold ring on the ground and offers it to you — then demands payment. Ignore and walk.
  • The friendship bracelet: Someone ties a bracelet on your wrist uninvited, then demands money for it. Don't let anyone touch your wrist.
  • The fake charity: Groups of young people with printed charity materials collecting donations. Not always legitimate — especially near tourist sites.
  • Fake ticket sellers: Outside the Eiffel Tower and Louvre, people selling "skip the line" tickets. All fake. Only buy through official websites.

Cultural Things That Trip Up Americans

  • Pharmacies are everywhere and excellent for everything from sunburn to headaches to blister pads. Look for the green cross. They are more accessible than clinics for minor issues.
  • Cafés don't rush you. Once you have a table, it's yours as long as you want it. The check won't appear until you ask — "L'addition, s'il vous plaît."
  • Lunch is the main meal in French culture. The midday formule (fixed-price lunch) at almost any restaurant is excellent value — often 3 courses for €18–25. Use it.
  • Shops close for lunch in some neighborhoods — typically 1–3pm. Don't assume a closed door means permanently closed.
  • Sunday hours are reduced across the board. Most shops open late (noon) or not at all. Plan Sunday accordingly.
  • Water at restaurants: Ask for une carafe d'eau (a pitcher of tap water) — it's free and perfectly fine. Bottled water is €4–6 a bottle at most restaurants.

Safety, Security & Areas
Paris is a safe city. Petty theft is real. Know the difference.
The honest baseline Paris ranked 74th out of 468 cities globally on the Numbeo safety index. Violent crime against tourists is extremely rare. The concern for travelers is pickpocketing — which is real, active, and entirely preventable with basic awareness. You are in far more danger of losing your phone than being hurt. This is a trade-off most travelers take every time.

Anti-Pickpocket Protocol

  • Bag worn across the body, in front. Not on your shoulder, not on your back. Crossbody, zipped, held against your body in crowds.
  • Phone stays in the bag. Not on the table, not in a back pocket, not dangling in your hand while walking. In the bag when not in active use.
  • Wallet in front pocket or bag. Never back pocket. Ever.
  • The pattern is always distraction then theft. Someone bumps you, asks for directions, creates commotion nearby. The moment you're distracted, the phone or wallet is gone. Being aware of this pattern is the entire defense.
  • Highest risk moments: Taking photos at tourist sites, boarding/exiting the Metro, walking through crowded markets, standing at the Eiffel Tower base.
  • Leave at the hotel: Passport originals (carry a photo on your phone), excess cash, expensive jewelry you're not wearing that day.

If Your Phone or Wallet Is Stolen

  • File a police report immediately — commissariat de police. You need this for insurance claims. The nearest to the hotel: Commissariat du 5ème, 4 Rue de la Montagne Sainte-Geneviève.
  • Call your bank immediately to freeze the card — numbers are on the back of the card; photograph these before you leave home.
  • Contact the US Embassy if your passport is stolen: +33 1 43 12 22 22.
  • For phone recovery: enable Find My (iPhone) or Find My Device (Android) before departure. These work in France.

Health & Medical

  • Travel insurance: Buy it. Medi-Assist or GeoBlue for US travelers. Covers medical evacuation, trip cancellation, and lost baggage.
  • Pharmacies (green cross) are everywhere in the 5th — they are the first stop for any minor issue. They speak English, dispense more without prescriptions than in the US, and are efficient.
  • Nearest hospital: Hôpital Pitié-Salpêtrière, 47-83 Bd de l'Hôpital (15-min walk from hotel).
  • July heat: Paris averages 25–28°C in July but occasional heat waves push higher. Stay hydrated, wear sunscreen, and take the afternoon Orangerie visit seriously — the oval rooms are air-conditioned.

Why These Choices — & What Else Is Out There
The reasoning behind every selection, and notable alternatives if the group wants to swap anything out.

Every place on this list was chosen for a specific reason tied to this group's profile: three women with a design and interior design sensibility, a love of food and fashion, a moderate pace, and a finite six days. The alternatives listed below are genuinely good — they were considered and left off for specific reasons, not because they're inferior.

Monument · Day 2
Sainte-Chapelle
★★★★★
4.6 · 48,973 reviews
Why It's Here

This is the one Paris experience that almost no one anticipates correctly. Every photograph understates it. The upper chapel is architecturally singular — a Gothic structure whose walls are almost entirely dissolved into stained glass, 15 windows 50 feet tall enclosing a space barely larger than a living room. For someone with Patty's design eye, it's the most concentrated architectural experience in the city. It's 12 minutes from the hotel. It costs €22. Virtually no one books it. That combination — extreme quality, low competition, perfect proximity — made it an automatic first inclusion.

Alternatives & Why Not
Notre-Dame CathedralLarger, more famous, currently being restored. Interior now reopened post-2019 fire. Worth seeing but the experience is diluted by scale and crowds. Sainte-Chapelle is more concentrated and more surprising.
Sacré-Cœur, MontmartreBeautiful hilltop setting but the basilica interior is ordinary. The neighborhood is overrun with tourists in July. Left off due to crowd density and lower design payoff.
Saint-SulpiceVast neoclassical church near Saint-Germain. Delacroix murals in the first chapel are extraordinary. Worth a quick stop if passing — no booking needed, free.
If They Want to Add Notre-Dame

Notre-Dame is an 8-minute walk from the hotel and earns an exterior visit on Day 1 regardless. The interior (recently restored) is accessible via timed booking at notredamedeparis.fr. It's on the route and worth 20 minutes inside — just don't replace Sainte-Chapelle with it.

Museum · Day 3
Musée de l'Orangerie
★★★★★
4.6 · 23,876 reviews
Why It's Here

The Orangerie was chosen over all other Impressionist options because it offers something the others can't: a purpose-built, spatially intentional experience. Monet designed the oval rooms himself — the dimensions, the zenithal skylights, the progression between the two rooms. You're not looking at art hung on walls; you're inside a constructed environment Monet specified. For an interior designer this distinction matters. The museum is also compact enough to see in 90 minutes without fatigue, and the Rousseau exhibition running until July 20 is included in the base ticket — a significant bonus.

Alternatives & Why Not
Musée Marmottan MonetThe largest Monet collection in the world, in a beautiful 16th-arrondissement mansion. Worth it for serious Monet devotees. Left off because it requires a dedicated half-day and is farther from the itinerary's geographic core.
Musée Jacquemart-AndréMagnificent private mansion turned museum, Italian Renaissance collection, extraordinary tea room. Genuinely overlooked gem. A strong alternative if the Orangerie is sold out.
Centre PompidouClosed for major renovation until 2030. Not currently an option.
Museum · Day 5
Musée d'Orsay
★★★★★
4.8 · 113,963 reviews
Why It's Here

Non-negotiable for this group. The Orsay holds the world's greatest Impressionist collection, and the building — a Beaux-Arts train station converted in 1986 — is architecturally extraordinary in its own right. The great clock faces, the nave, the height of the former station hall. It was placed on Friday rather than earlier in the week because the Paris by Mouth food tour anchors the morning, and having Sainte-Chapelle and the Orangerie earlier means arriving at the Orsay with context rather than cold. Pairing it with a leisurely afternoon on Rue Mouffetard keeps the day from over-programming.

Alternatives & Why Not
Musée RodinBeautiful mansion and sculpture garden with The Thinker and The Kiss. Worth adding if there's a free afternoon — no timed entry required, Museum Pass covers it. Could replace the Galeries Lafayette rooftop on Day 3.
Musée PicassoIn the Marais, strong collection across all Picasso periods. Museum Pass covers it. Could be added on the Marais day without disrupting the itinerary.
Musée de ClunyMedieval art and Roman ruins, 5-minute walk from the hotel. The Lady and the Unicorn tapestries are extraordinary. Worth 90 minutes on any free afternoon — and it's steps from the front door.
Note for 2026

Major renovation works in the Orsay reception area run June 10–October 5, 2026. No groups permitted. Individual visitors fully welcome. Use Entrance C (pre-booked visitors) and allow extra time for modified entry flow.

Museum · Day 3 · Free
Petit Palais
★★★★★
4.7 · 24,890 reviews
Why It's Here

The Petit Palais earns its slot because of a simple equation: zero cost, no queue, extraordinary interior, and a courtyard that has stopped interior designers cold for 125 years. Its inclusion is also a statement about the itinerary's philosophy — that the best experiences in Paris are frequently the overlooked ones, and that a free afternoon in a magnificent Beaux-Arts building is better than an hour in a tourist queue. It pairs geographically with the Orangerie and Galeries Lafayette on Day 3, requiring no additional transit.

Alternatives & Why Not
Grand PalaisThe Petit Palais's larger neighbor. Hosts major temporary exhibitions but charges €15–20 per show. The Petit Palais permanent collection is free and architecturally superior. If there's a specific Grand Palais exhibition running in July, it may be worth adding.
Musée des Arts DécoratifsDecorative arts and fashion museum adjacent to the Louvre. Extremely relevant for Patty's professional interests — furniture, textiles, historic interior design. A strong swap for the Petit Palais if she wants more design-specific content.
Museum · Day 6
The Louvre
★★★★★
4.7 · 367,614 reviews
Why It's Here

The Louvre is on the list because it was specifically requested, and because it belongs on any Paris itinerary regardless. Its placement on the last full day is deliberate: by Saturday the group has already seen the Orangerie and Orsay, giving them Impressionist and art historical context they'll lack on Day 1. They also arrive knowing exactly what they want to see rather than feeling obligated to see everything. The three-target strategy — Mona Lisa, Winged Victory, Venus de Milo — is designed to make the visit feel focused rather than overwhelming. The Louvre is inexhaustible; the goal is to leave satisfied, not exhausted.

What to Skip Inside
The Egyptian Antiquities wingExtraordinary but requires a separate 2-hour commitment. Save for a dedicated return trip.
The Dutch/Flemish galleriesVermeer, Rembrandt — world-class, but not the right entry point for a first or casual Louvre visit. These reward slow return visits.
The Richelieu wing (sculpture)French and Northern European sculpture. Beautiful but typically skipped on a first visit. With a private guide using the Richelieu entrance (available since Jan 2026), this becomes accessible on a curated path.
Garden & Arcade · Days 3 & 6 · Free
Jardin du Palais Royal
★★★★★
4.7 · 9,670 reviews
Why It's Here — Twice

The Palais Royal appears twice in the itinerary because it's worth two visits and each serves a different purpose. On Day 3 it's a lunch anchor between the Petit Palais and Galeries Lafayette — the garden provides a natural pause, and the arcade restaurants deliver a quiet meal between active sightseeing. On Day 6 it's the post-Louvre debrief and final shopping loop — the arcade shops (vintage eyewear, antique medals, small jewelers) are exactly the kind of places Patty and Patty's friends will find rewarding. The Buren columns are an architectural conversation piece regardless of which side of the debate they land on.

Alternatives & Why Not
Jardin du Luxembourg10-minute walk from the hotel, one of Paris's great gardens. Beautiful in July. A perfect afternoon alternative if the group wants a slower day — bring food from Rue Mouffetard and sit in the famous green metal chairs.
Tuileries GardenAlready on Day 3 as a walkthrough between the Orangerie and the Palais Royal. The full Tuileries is worth a dedicated hour if there's appetite for more garden time.
Restaurant · Monday Dinner
Brasserie des Prés
★★★★★
4.7 · 8,367 reviews
Why It's Here

The first dinner sets the tone for the whole trip. Brasserie des Prés was chosen because the location — the Cour du Commerce Saint-André, one of the oldest covered passages in Paris (1776) — is a Paris experience in itself, not just a restaurant. The passage is beautiful, the room is beautiful, the food is classic French at a high level. After a transatlantic flight, the group needs a dinner that feels like Paris without requiring adventurousness or decision fatigue. This is that dinner. It's also 15 minutes from the hotel on foot, eliminating any navigation complexity on the first night.

Alternatives if Unavailable
AllardClassic Saint-Germain bistro on Rue de l'Éperon, classic since 1931. Duck with olives, escargot. Similar neighborhood, similar quality. Backup if des Prés is booked.
Le Comptoir du RelaisChef Yves Camdeborde's legendary Saint-Germain bistro. Exceptional food, notoriously difficult to book. Worth trying if they can get a reservation — it's in the same neighborhood.
LapérouseHistoric restaurant on the quai with private dining rooms etched by 18th-century courtesans with their diamonds. Theatrical, expensive, unforgettable. A special occasion option.
Restaurant · Tuesday & Saturday Dinner
Brasserie Bellanger
★★★★★
4.7 · 6,041 reviews
Why It's Here — Twice

Bellanger is on the list twice because it earned it. The food is exceptional, the room delivers, and the staff are warm in a way that's not guaranteed at Paris brasseries. It was booked twice (Tuesday and Saturday) because the last dinner of any trip should go to the best place encountered during it — and Bellanger is very likely to be that place. If another restaurant on the trip surprises them, the Saturday reservation can be changed. But the safety of knowing the last dinner is secured at a place this good is worth the double booking.

Alternatives if It Can't Be Booked
Le Bon George9th arrondissement, similar aesthetic — beautiful room, classic French, warm staff. Slightly easier to book than Bellanger in July.
Brasserie LutetiaInside the Hôtel Lutetia, 6th arrondissement. More grand, more formal, more expensive. Worth it for a special occasion dinner.
Au Passage11th arrondissement natural wine bar and small plates. Excellent, different format — sharing dishes, no tablecloths. A strong alternative for the Tuesday slot if Bellanger is unavailable.
Restaurant · Thursday Dinner
Bouillon République
★★★★★
4.7 · 37,406 reviews
Why It's Here

37,000 Google reviews at 4.7 stars is not noise — it's consensus. Bouillon République was chosen because the bouillon format is a specifically Parisian institution with no American equivalent, making it a genuine cultural experience rather than just a meal. The historic room, the democratic pricing, the volume and energy — this is Paris feeding itself, not Paris performing for tourists. The bone marrow and duck parfait are the correct orders, and the mille-feuille is the correct dessert. It's also the most affordable dinner on the list, which creates useful budget space for wine.

Alternatives if It Can't Be Booked
Bouillon ChartierThe original Paris bouillon, open since 1896. No reservations taken — pure walk-in, which creates a wait. More historic room than République, more chaotic service. Worth the experience if Bouillon République is unavailable.
Chez JanouMarais Provençal bistro. Great for the Marais day if staying in the neighborhood for dinner. Different format — more intimate, Southern French.
Fashion Boutique · Le Marais
A L'O — 1905
★★★★★
4.9 · 656 reviews
Why It's Here

A L'O was chosen over the obvious luxury alternatives (Rue Saint-Honoré flagships, Galeries Lafayette designer floors) because the pieces are genuinely unavailable in the US market, the prices are rational rather than luxury-tier, and the shopkeeper Chris provides an experience that no department store can replicate. For an interior designer who values craftsmanship and provenance, French workwear brands like Vetra represent exactly the aesthetic intelligence she brings to her own work. The 4.9-star rating on 656 reviews is driven almost entirely by people mentioning Chris by name — a signal that the experience is personal and consistent.

Additional Shopping Options
Merci Concept StoreBoulevard Beaumarchais, Marais. Large, curated multi-brand store with furniture, fashion, and home goods. Design-relevant for Patty's work. Worth 45 minutes if time allows.
Rue Saint-HonoréThe luxury flagships — Hermès, Chanel, Saint Laurent. Worth walking even without buying. The Hermès flagship at 24 Rue du Faubourg Saint-Honoré is architecturally significant and the sales experience is worth noting.
Le Bon MarchéThe grande dame of Paris department stores, 7th arrondissement. More curated than Galeries Lafayette, stronger in home goods and interior design objects. Particularly relevant for Patty's professional interests.
What Was Left Off — and Why
Considered & Excluded
Montmartre / Sacré-Cœur — Beautiful neighborhood, unremarkable basilica, extreme July tourist density. The residential streets below are worth wandering on a quieter trip but not worth the crowd exposure in July.

Versailles (as a given) — Included as an option but not a default. A full day, heavy crowds in July, and it displaces an entire neighborhood day. The Marais is the more memorable choice for this group.

Eiffel Tower elevator — Requested as a "see it, not hang out at it" experience, which is exactly how this guide treats it. 90 minutes of July queuing for a view you already know from photographs is not the right trade.

Michelin-starred restaurants — Deliberately excluded. The format (long tasting menus, formal pacing) doesn't fit a group that wants conversation, flexibility, and a bottle of Burgundy. The four brasseries deliver better dining experiences for this trip's dynamic.
If There's a Free Afternoon
Musée de Cluny — 5-minute walk from the hotel. Medieval art and actual Roman ruins. The Lady and the Unicorn tapestries are extraordinary. Museum Pass covers it.

Musée Rodin — Left Bank sculpture garden with The Thinker and The Kiss. 45 minutes, Museum Pass covers it. Strong addition to Day 3 or 5.

Le Bon Marché — 7th arrondissement. The best-curated department store in Paris for design-oriented shoppers. Excellent home goods floor.

Jardin du Luxembourg — 10-minute walk from the hotel. Paris's most beautiful garden. Bring cheese and wine from Rue Mouffetard and spend an hour in the green chairs.
